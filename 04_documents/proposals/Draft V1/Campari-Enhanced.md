### **Focus Areas for Strategic Impact**

#### **1. Talent Attraction and Diverse Pipelines**

This focus area addresses the critical need to expand Campari's reach into professional communities that have traditionally been underrepresented in the beverage industry and corporate leadership. The ultimate objective is to increase the diversity of your talent pipeline while maintaining the highest quality standards and ensuring long-term organizational benefit.

#### **2. Development of Inclusive Leadership Competencies**

Leadership plays a fundamental role in shaping organizational culture and determining the success of any diversity and inclusion initiative. Leaders who understand the value of diverse perspectives, recognize their own unconscious biases, and have the skills to build psychologically safe teams where all voices are heard create environments where diverse talent can thrive. Our partnership provides targeted support for leadership development that addresses the specific competencies required to manage diverse teams effectively. This includes helping leaders understand the business case for diversity, recognize and address unconscious bias in their decision-making processes, create inclusive team environments where psychological safety enables open dialogue, and develop the coaching and mentoring skills necessary to develop talent from diverse backgrounds. Through workshops, coaching, and strategic discussions, we help build a leadership population that views diversity not as a compliance issue or nice-to-have, but as a strategic driver of organizational performance and innovation.

#### **3. Strengthening Organizational Culture**

Organizational culture is the foundation upon which all diversity and inclusion initiatives succeed or fail. Even the most well-designed programs will fall short if the underlying culture does not support and reinforce inclusive behaviors and a genuine sense of belonging for all employees. Strengthening organizational culture in the context of diversity means creating environments where differences are genuinely valued, where people feel they can bring their authentic selves to work, and where decisions are made with consideration for diverse perspectives and impacts.

This focus area encompasses a range of activities designed to shift cultural norms and practices to support inclusion. It includes facilitating conversations about what inclusion means in your organization, creating or strengthening employee resource groups that foster community and belonging, reviewing organizational policies and practices for unintended discriminatory impacts, implementing communication standards that promote inclusive language, and creating rituals and moments that celebrate and reinforce inclusive values. The goal is to move beyond surface-level diversity metrics to create a culture where inclusion is embedded in how work gets done and where all employees feel a genuine sense of belonging and opportunity.

#### **4. Positioning as an Inclusive Employer Brand**

In the modern business environment, employer brand is a critical strategic asset that directly influences your ability to attract top talent, particularly from communities that have historically faced barriers in corporate environments. An authentic commitment to diversity and inclusion, communicated through your employer brand, resonates particularly strongly with millennial and Gen Z talent, increasingly diverse consumer segments, and socially conscious investors. Positioning Campari as a leader in diversity and inclusion requires not only implementing strong policies and programs internally but also effectively communicating your commitment and demonstrating your progress to external stakeholders.

This focus area involves aligning your diversity and inclusion strategy with your broader brand messaging, ensuring that your employer brand authentically reflects your organizational values and commitment to inclusion, and communicating your diversity initiatives and progress through channels that reach diverse talent communities. This includes reviewing internal and external communications through an inclusion lens, developing compelling narratives about your diversity journey, creating content that showcases the experiences of diverse employees, and ensuring that recruitment materials authentically represent the diversity of your organization. When executed strategically, employer brand positioning creates a virtuous cycle where authentic commitment to inclusion attracts diverse talent, diverse teams drive innovation and better decision-making, and these outcomes reinforce your positioning as an inclusive employer.

## **Front 1: Diversity Strategy and Governance**

### **Foundational Element: Building Strategic Architecture**

The strategy and governance front forms the foundation for all other diversity and inclusion work. Without a clear strategic framework that articulates why diversity matters for your business, what you're trying to achieve, and how you'll measure progress, individual initiatives remain disconnected and lack the alignment necessary for sustained impact. This front focuses on developing the strategic architecture and governance structures that enable an organization to build comprehensive, integrated diversity and inclusion approaches.

Work in this front typically includes conducting a comprehensive assessment of current diversity maturity across your organization. This assessment examines the current state of diversity in your workforce, the existing diversity initiatives and programs, organizational readiness for change, key barriers and obstacles, and opportunities for impact. The assessment provides a baseline from which to measure progress and helps identify which areas should be prioritized. Based on the assessment, we facilitate strategic planning processes that help your leadership team define the pillars or focus areas of your diversity strategy, connect these to business objectives, and develop a compelling vision for what your organization will look like as you advance your diversity agenda.

Defining strategic pillars involves more than naming priorities—it requires articulating clearly how each pillar connects to business objectives, what outcomes you're trying to drive, and what organizational capabilities you need to develop. For example, if "talent acquisition diversity" is a pillar, it requires clarity about what diversity metrics you're targeting, what barriers you need to address in your recruitment process, what new sourcing strategies you'll implement, and how you'll measure success. This clarity enables more effective resource allocation and helps all organizational leaders understand how their areas contribute to the broader diversity strategy.

We also work with your organization to structure governance frameworks that enable sustained progress. This includes defining clear accountability for diversity initiatives, establishing cross-functional teams that break down organizational silos, creating regular review processes to assess progress against objectives, and building mechanisms for organizational learning and continuous improvement. Effective governance ensures that diversity and inclusion doesn't depend on individual champions but becomes embedded in organizational systems and accountability structures.

Creating indicators and establishing robust measurement and acompanhamento (tracking) systems is critical for accountability and continuous improvement. This involves defining both outcome metrics (e.g., diversity of workforce by level and function) and process metrics (e.g., pipeline metrics, program participation). We help you establish baselines, set targets, and create regular reporting mechanisms so that progress is visible and adjustments can be made when needed.

## **Front 2: Talent Acquisition and Diverse Pipeline Development**

### **Strategic Imperative: Accessing Global Talent Communities**

Talent acquisition is one of the areas where diversity and inclusion efforts can have the most immediate and visible impact. When your recruitment process proactively reaches diverse talent communities, actively removes barriers to consideration, and attracts high-quality diverse candidates, it creates the foundation for diversity at all organizational levels. This front focuses on transforming your recruitment approach to expand the talent pool you access while maintaining—and often improving—the quality of your hiring.

The starting point for this work is typically a comprehensive review of your current recruitment processes and strategies. We examine how you currently source candidates, what attracts candidates to apply, what happens in your interview and assessment process, and what factors influence final hiring decisions. This review often reveals hidden barriers in your process—things that aren't intentionally discriminatory but have the unintended effect of limiting diversity. For example, relying primarily on employee referrals may perpetuate homogeneity if your current workforce is not diverse. Or job descriptions that include "requirements" that are actually nice-to-haves may inadvertently exclude diverse candidates who bring relevant experience from different paths.

We also help you develop new sourcing strategies that proactively connect with diverse professional communities and networks. This might include partnerships with professional organizations focused on underrepresented groups, targeted outreach to universities with strong diverse alumni networks, connections with diversity recruiting firms, and engagement with community organizations. The goal is to build relationships with diverse professional communities so that your organization becomes known as an employer of choice for diverse talent. This requires commitment, consistency, and authentic follow-through on your diversity commitments.

Structured interview and assessment approaches are critical for reducing bias in hiring decisions. We help you develop interview guides and assessment tools that ask all candidates the same questions, evaluate responses against clear criteria, and document decision-making processes in ways that reduce the likelihood of unconscious bias influencing outcomes. We also provide training for recruiters, hiring managers, and interviewers to help them recognize unconscious bias and implement structured approaches that counteract it.

Creating compelling value propositions for diverse talent is increasingly important. Diverse candidates—particularly those from groups that have faced barriers in corporate environments—are evaluating employers not just on compensation and benefits but on authentic commitment to diversity and inclusion. We help you develop messaging that authentically communicates your diversity story, your commitment to inclusion, your programs that support diverse talent development, and the experiences of your diverse employees. This messaging should be authentic and backed up by real organizational commitment and policies.

### **Recruitment Process Review and Barrier Removal**

Many organizations don't realize that their recruitment processes contain hidden barriers that inadvertently limit diversity. These might be in job descriptions that emphasize specific credentials when diverse candidates bring equivalent experience through different paths, in sourcing strategies that rely heavily on homogeneous networks, in interview approaches that advantage certain communication styles, or in assessment tools that may have disparate impact on certain groups. We conduct comprehensive reviews of your recruitment processes to identify these barriers and develop strategies to address them.

### **Pipeline Development Through Strategic Relationships**

Diverse talent doesn't necessarily come to you—you often need to proactively build relationships with diverse professional communities and networks. This might include partnering with diversity-focused professional organizations, building relationships with universities that produce diverse talent, connecting with community organizations that support workforce development, or working with diverse recruiting specialists. Building these relationships requires consistency and authentic follow-through on your diversity commitments.

### **Support in Key Strategic Positions**

Some positions are particularly critical for organizational perception of diversity and inclusion commitment. We provide targeted support for recruiting diverse candidates for these positions, including executive search support, interview preparation for internal diverse candidates being developed for advancement, and ensuring that these key positions are identified as recruitment priorities.

### **Organizational Communication About Inclusive Recruitment**

Even the most well-designed diversity initiatives will not be sustained if organizational culture does not support and value inclusion. Culture includes the shared values, norms, and behaviors that characterize your organization—how decisions are made, whose voices are heard, what behaviors are rewarded and punished, and whether people feel they belong. Transforming culture to support inclusion is a long-term effort that requires consistent reinforcement, visible commitment from leadership, and alignment of organizational systems and practices.

Work in this front includes developing and facilitating organizational conversations about inclusion and belonging. These conversations help your organization move beyond surface-level diversity metrics to explore what inclusion really means in your context, what barriers to belonging currently exist, and what needs to change to create an organization where all employees feel valued and can bring their authentic selves to work. We facilitate safe spaces for these conversations, help leaders listen without defensiveness, and identify opportunities for cultural change that emerge from these dialogues.

Developing inclusive leadership capabilities is another critical element of this front. We support development of your leadership population through workshops, coaching, and ongoing dialogue focused on the competencies required to lead diverse teams and create inclusive environments. These competencies include understanding unconscious bias and how it shapes decision-making, building psychological safety on teams, developing talent from diverse backgrounds, making decisions that consider diverse perspectives, and addressing issues of exclusion or discrimination when they arise.

These groups provide space for employees from underrepresented groups to build community, provide support to one another, offer input to organizational leadership on inclusion initiatives, and serve as talent development resources. We help you think through the governance of these groups, develop leadership pipelines for group leaders, define objectives and agendas, and integrate these groups strategically into your broader diversity and inclusion efforts.

Supporting diversity-related internal programs might include designing and facilitating conversations about difficult topics such as unconscious bias, racial equity, gender equity, or inclusion of people with disabilities. It might include developing employee mentoring or sponsorship programs that intentionally develop diverse talent. It might include designing and implementing policies that reflect commitment to inclusion, such as flexible work policies, non-discrimination policies, or policies related to parental leave, gender identity, or religious accommodation. The goal is to create an organizational ecosystem where inclusion is embedded in systems, processes, and practices.

## **Lectures: Awareness-Building and Inspiration**

### **Overview and Strategic Purpose**

Lectures are focused, inspirational sessions designed to build organizational awareness and understanding of diversity and inclusion topics. Unlike training (which focuses on skill development) or workshops (which are interactive and hands-on), lectures provide content and perspective-sharing that helps create awareness, shift mindsets, and inspire commitment to inclusion. Lectures are particularly effective for reaching large groups of employees, creating organizational moments that signal leadership commitment to diversity, and introducing concepts or frameworks that can ground subsequent work.

We work with your team to tailor lecture content to your organizational context, your audience, your specific priorities, and your diversity maturity level. We examine concrete business benefits of diversity and help organizational leaders commit to diversity as a business priority rather than a compliance or HR issue.

**Inclusive Leadership in Practice**: This session provides leaders with frameworks and tools for leading diverse teams effectively. The lecture covers inclusive leadership competencies, examines common challenges leaders face in managing diverse teams, addresses unconscious bias in leadership decision-making, and provides practical strategies for building psychological safety, developing diverse talent, and making inclusive decisions. This lecture is particularly valuable for leadership populations and helps build awareness of what inclusive leadership requires.

**Unconscious Bias in Organizations**: This lecture helps employees and leaders understand unconscious bias—how all humans have unconscious associations and biases, how these biases can influence organizational decisions (hiring, promotions, performance evaluation), and what strategies can help mitigate bias in decision-making. Rather than making people feel guilty about having unconscious biases, this lecture helps them understand that biases are normal and that organizations can implement systems and practices that reduce the impact of bias on organizational outcomes.

**Racial Equity in Organizations**: This lecture addresses racial equity directly, examining historical and contemporary racial inequities, helping organizations understand why racial equity is a business and moral imperative, examining how racial inequities manifest in organizational systems and practices, and providing frameworks for advancing racial equity. The lecture helps teams work across generational differences and appreciate diverse perspectives and work styles.

The lecture emphasizes that diversity of ability makes organizations more innovative and that accommodations for employees with disabilities benefit all employees.

**Organizational Culture and Belonging**: This lecture explores the relationship between organizational culture and diversity and inclusion, helps organizations understand what creates sense of belonging, examines how organizational norms and practices can either support or undermine belonging, and helps leaders understand their role in creating culturally inclusive environments.

# **Section 8 — Expected Results and Organizational Impact**

## **Strategic Value of Comprehensive Diversity and Inclusion**

The implementation of a thoughtfully designed, strategically aligned diversity, equity, and inclusion agenda creates significant value for organizations—both in terms of organizational culture and employee experience, and in terms of business performance and competitive advantage. This section outlines the key result areas and organizational benefits that typically emerge from a sustained commitment to diversity and inclusion work.

---

## **Strengthening and Deepening Organizational Culture**

### **From Transaction to Belonging**

Organizational culture represents the shared values, beliefs, norms, and behaviors that characterize your organization. It influences how decisions are made, whose voices are heard, what behaviors are rewarded and punished, and fundamentally affects whether employees feel they belong and can thrive. A strong diversity and inclusion agenda creates measurable shifts in organizational culture, moving from organizations where diversity exists but may not be valued, to organizations where inclusion is embedded in culture and where all employees feel a genuine sense of belonging.

The cultural shifts that typically emerge from diversity and inclusion work include increased respect for differences and diverse perspectives, stronger cross-functional relationships across demographic groups, increased openness to new ideas and approaches, greater willingness to address difficult conversations and conflicts, and greater sense of psychological safety where people feel comfortable being themselves at work. These cultural shifts create more engaging, collaborative, innovative workplaces where employees feel valued and motivated.

### **Measurable Cultural Indicators**

Organizations that implement comprehensive diversity and inclusion agendas typically see improvements in cultural indicators tracked through employee engagement surveys: increased sense of belonging, increased feeling of being valued for contributions, increased trust in leadership, increased confidence that the organization values diversity, increased willingness to voice different perspectives, and increased perception that the organization is truly committed to inclusion. Over time, these cultural improvements translate to better employee engagement, lower turnover, and higher organizational performance.

---

## **Development of More Capable and Inclusive Leadership Population**

### **From Management to Inclusive Leadership**

Leadership development in the context of diversity and inclusion creates a qualitatively different leadership population. Leaders who understand inclusive leadership not just as a values proposition but as a business imperative, who have developed awareness of unconscious bias in their own decision-making, who have developed skills in building psychologically safe teams, and who actively develop talent from diverse backgrounds create fundamentally different organizational environments and results.

Organizations that invest in inclusive leadership development typically see several outcomes: leaders who are more capable of managing complex, global, diverse teams effectively; leaders who demonstrate greater emotional intelligence and self-awareness; leaders who are more effective at building team cohesion across differences; leaders who are more effective at identifying and developing high-potential talent from diverse backgrounds; and leaders who more effectively leverage diversity for innovation and problem-solving.

### **Succession Planning and Leadership Bench Strength**

One significant benefit of inclusive leadership development is strengthened succession planning. Organizations that intentionally develop diverse talent, provide mentoring and sponsorship for underrepresented leaders, and create inclusive environments where diverse leaders can thrive typically have stronger leadership pipelines with greater diversity at all levels. This improves organizational resilience and flexibility and ensures access to the broadest possible talent pool for future leadership roles.

---

## **Increased Attraction and Retention of Diverse Talent**

### **Expanding Talent Pool and Recruitment Quality**

Organizations that actively work to increase diversity in recruitment typically see improvements in both the diversity of their candidate pools and the quality of candidates attracted. This happens for several reasons: by proactively reaching into diverse professional communities, organizations access talent that may not otherwise know about their opportunities; by removing barriers in recruitment processes, organizations enable strong candidates to advance through their recruitment processes; and by authentically communicating commitment to inclusion, organizations attract candidates who value diversity and are looking for inclusive employers.

Over time, these improvements in recruitment translate to more diverse workforces with broader ranges of experience, perspective, and background. This diversity of talent pool improves organizational capability to serve diverse customer bases, enter new markets, and respond to evolving business challenges.

### **Retention and Engagement of Diverse Employees**

Beyond attraction, organizations that implement comprehensive diversity and inclusion agendas typically see improvements in retention of diverse employees. This happens because diverse employees are more likely to stay in organizations where they feel valued, where they see pathways for advancement, where they have mentoring and sponsorship support, and where they see organizational commitment to diversity reflected in policies, practices, and leadership. Organizations often see reductions in turnover among diverse employees when they implement comprehensive diversity agendas, which improves continuity and organizational capability.

---

## **Enhanced Innovation and Problem-Solving Capability**

### **Diversity Driving Cognitive Diversity and Innovation**

Research increasingly demonstrates that diverse teams and organizations are more innovative and better at problem-solving. This happens because diversity of background, experience, and perspective brings different ways of thinking about problems, different networks and information sources, and different approaches to solutions. When organizations have psychologically safe environments where diverse perspectives are genuinely sought and valued, teams are able to leverage this cognitive diversity for more creative problem-solving and innovation.

Organizations that implement comprehensive diversity and inclusion agendas typically see improvements in innovation metrics: increased number of new ideas generated, increased diversity of ideas coming from different parts of the organization, greater willingness to challenge conventional thinking, more effective cross-functional collaboration, and better solutions to complex problems. These improvements in innovation capability translate directly to competitive advantage.

### **Market and Customer Responsiveness**

Beyond internal innovation, organizations with diverse teams often develop better understanding of and ability to serve diverse customer and market segments. Diverse teams bring authentic understanding of diverse market segments, diverse networks and relationships, and diverse perspectives on customer needs and preferences. This enables organizations to develop products and services that more effectively serve diverse markets, to communicate more effectively to diverse audiences, and to respond more effectively to changing market conditions.

---

## **Improved Decision-Making and Organizational Performance**

### **Quality of Organizational Decisions**

Research demonstrates that diverse teams, when operating in psychologically safe environments, make better decisions than homogeneous teams. This happens because diverse perspectives help teams see blind spots, consider alternative approaches, and identify risks or opportunities that more homogeneous groups might miss. Organizations that build inclusive cultures where diverse perspectives are genuinely valued typically see improvements in quality of organizational decision-making.

### **Business Performance Metrics**

Organizations that implement comprehensive diversity and inclusion agendas and that have successfully built genuinely inclusive cultures typically see measurable improvements in business performance metrics. Research demonstrates correlations between diversity and inclusion and various performance metrics: revenue growth, profitability, employee productivity, customer satisfaction, and stock performance. While these correlations are influenced by many factors, the pattern suggests that diversity and inclusion contribute meaningfully to organizational performance.

## **Strengthening Brand and Market Positioning**

### **Employer Brand and Talent Attraction**

In today's market, your positioning as an inclusive employer is increasingly important to your ability to attract diverse talent, particularly from younger generations and from communities that have historically faced barriers in employment. Organizations that authentically commit to diversity and effectively communicate this commitment typically see strengthened employer brand positioning, broader reach into diverse talent markets, and improved ability to attract high-potential diverse talent.

### **Corporate Reputation and Stakeholder Relationships**

Beyond talent markets, many organizations find that commitment to diversity and inclusion strengthens their corporate reputation and relationships with various stakeholders: diverse communities, customers who value corporate social responsibility, investors who increasingly consider diversity as a governance and performance factor, and employees who value working for socially responsible organizations. In an increasingly socially conscious business environment, authentic commitment to diversity can be a significant competitive advantage.

## **Evolution of Organizational Governance and Accountability**

### **Formalizing Diversity as a Business Imperative**

Organizations that implement comprehensive diversity and inclusion agendas typically formalize diversity and inclusion as part of organizational governance and accountability structures. This might include establishing board-level oversight of diversity progress, including diversity metrics in executive compensation, establishing diversity as a functional responsibility across all business functions, and creating regular reporting mechanisms that make diversity progress visible.

This formalization signals organizational commitment and ensures that diversity remains a priority even as business conditions change or leadership shifts. It embeds diversity into organizational decision-making and ensures that diversity is not dependent on individual champions.

### **Measurement, Learning, and Continuous Improvement**

Organizations that implement comprehensive diversity agendas establish measurement and monitoring systems that enable continuous learning and improvement. By measuring progress against defined objectives, establishing baselines and targets, and regularly reporting results, organizations create visibility and accountability. This measurement also enables organizational learning—understanding what strategies are most effective, what barriers exist, and where adjustments are needed.

### **From Programs to Embedded Practice**

The ultimate goal of diversity and inclusion work is not to implement programs, but to embed diversity and inclusion into organizational culture, systems, and decision-making in ways that are sustainable over time. Organizations that do this work most effectively move from viewing diversity as a separate "program" to viewing it as integral to how the organization operates.

This evolution happens when: diversity metrics are integrated into organizational performance metrics, leadership accountability for diversity is embedded in performance management, diverse perspectives are genuinely sought in organizational decision-making, inclusive practices are embedded in organizational systems and processes, and continuous learning and improvement in diversity and inclusion becomes normal organizational practice.

# **Section 9 — Working Methodology and Approach**

## **Integrated Approach Balancing Three Critical Dimensions**

DiverCidade Hub's approach to diversity and inclusion consulting combines work across three interconnected dimensions: strategic direction-setting, practical implementation of initiatives, and organizational development and culture building. Our methodology recognizes that sustainable diversity and inclusion requires progress across all three dimensions simultaneously, and that these dimensions reinforce one another.

---

## **Dimension 1: Strategic Direction and Alignment**

### **Clarity on Why and What**

Much diversity and inclusion work fails not because the initiatives are poorly designed, but because there isn't sufficient clarity about why diversity matters for the organization, what the organization is trying to achieve, and how specific initiatives connect to broader organizational objectives. Our work on the strategy dimension focuses on creating this clarity.

This includes helping your leadership team understand the business case for diversity specific to your organization and industry, articulating a clear vision for what your organization will look like as you advance your diversity agenda, identifying specific strategic priorities that align to business objectives and organizational capabilities, defining clear objectives and key results for each priority area, and ensuring alignment between diversity objectives and broader organizational strategy.

We facilitate strategic discussions with your leadership team that go beyond diversity to connect diversity to core business challenges and opportunities. For example, if your organization is seeking to enter new markets, we help your leadership understand how diversity in your workforce and leadership can enable market entry. If your organization is struggling with innovation, we help your leadership understand how diverse teams drive innovation. This connection to business strategy is essential for sustaining commitment and resources.

### **Governance and Accountability Frameworks**

Clear strategy requires clear governance and accountability. We help you establish governance structures—typically a diversity steering committee or leadership council that includes senior leaders, functional heads, and sometimes board representation. We help define clear accountability for diversity results, create regular review mechanisms that make progress visible, establish escalation processes for addressing barriers, and create processes for organizational learning and continuous improvement.

---

## **Dimension 2: Initiative Implementation and Program Development**

### **From Strategy to Action**

While strategy is essential, it means nothing without effective implementation. Our work on this dimension focuses on translating strategic direction into specific initiatives, programs, and projects that drive concrete progress.

This includes designing specific programs and initiatives aligned to your strategic priorities, developing detailed project plans and timelines, identifying necessary resources and capability, implementing initiatives with attention to quality and impact, and monitoring implementation to ensure initiatives are delivering intended results.

We work hands-on with your team on implementation—this is not consulting that hands off a report and walks away, but deep partnership in developing and implementing initiatives. This might include helping design recruitment processes, facilitating planning sessions for employee resource groups, developing content for leadership development, helping think through policy changes, or supporting recruitment directly.

### **Building Organizational Capability Through Implementation**

An important aspect of our implementation work is building organizational capability so that your team can sustain progress beyond our partnership. We work in partnership with your internal teams—HR, communications, operations—to develop their capabilities while we work together on implementation. This might include training your HR team in new recruitment approaches, developing your internal facilitators to deliver leadership development, or building your communications team's capability to develop diversity messaging.

---

## **Dimension 3: Organizational Development and Culture Building**

### **Systems, Culture, and People Alignment**

Sustainable diversity and inclusion requires alignment of three elements: organizational systems and processes, organizational culture and norms, and people capabilities and mindsets. Our work on this dimension focuses on ensuring that these three elements are aligned and mutually reinforcing.

This includes reviewing organizational systems and processes to ensure they support diversity objectives, facilitating conversations that help shift cultural norms to value inclusion, and developing leadership and individual capabilities to support inclusive behaviors. For example, a new inclusive recruitment process (system) only works if hiring managers understand why it's important and are committed to it (mindset), and if organizational culture values diverse perspectives (culture).

### **Facilitating Organizational Conversations**

Much of organizational development happens through facilitated conversations. We help facilitate conversations about what inclusion means in your organization, conversations about barriers to inclusion that exist in your culture, conversations about how to leverage diversity for business benefit, and difficult conversations about diversity challenges or issues. These conversations help build organizational understanding and commitment.

### **Change Management and Overcoming Resistance**

Any organizational transformation encounters some resistance. We help your organization manage change effectively by understanding sources of resistance, addressing concerns, building organizational case for change, celebrating progress and building momentum, and helping navigate the discomfort that often accompanies significant change.

---

## **Integrated Approach: How These Dimensions Work Together**

These three dimensions work together in practice. Strategic clarity (Dimension 1) guides which initiatives to prioritize (Dimension 2), while successful implementation (Dimension 2) builds organizational confidence and momentum for culture change (Dimension 3). Cultural shifts (Dimension 3) create foundations for new initiatives (Dimension 2) and provide evidence for the strategic importance of diversity (Dimension 1).

Our work flows among these three dimensions throughout our partnership. In early months, we might spend more time on strategy and planning, then move into more intensive implementation work, while always maintaining attention to culture and organizational development. As partnership progresses, we might cycle back to strategy refinement based on implementation learning, while continuing to support implementation and organizational development.

# **Section 10 — Partnership Stages and Journey**

## **Evolution Through Four Key Partnership Stages**

While every organization's diversity journey is unique, most organizations move through several identifiable stages in developing and implementing their diversity and inclusion agenda. Understanding these stages helps set realistic expectations and focus partnership efforts at each stage.

---

## **Stage 1: Diagnosis and Context Understanding**

### **Purpose and Activities**

The first stage of partnership is focused on building deep understanding of your organizational context, current state, challenges, and opportunities. This is foundational work that informs everything that follows. This stage typically takes 4-8 weeks and is characterized by significant listening and learning.

During this stage, we conduct interviews with organizational leaders and stakeholders to understand their perspectives on diversity, current priorities, and challenges. We examine existing diversity initiatives and assess their effectiveness. We review policies and processes for potential bias or barriers. We may conduct focus groups or surveys with employees to understand employee experiences related to diversity and inclusion. We analyze workforce data to understand current diversity metrics and trends. We benchmark your organization against peers to understand competitive positioning on diversity.

### **Typical Deliverables**

This stage typically produces: a comprehensive organizational assessment identifying current state, key strengths and opportunities, priority recommendations for improvement, and often a proposed roadmap for diversity advancement.

### **Outcomes and Next Steps**

By the end of this stage, your leadership team has a clear understanding of where the organization stands on diversity and inclusion, clarity about key opportunities and challenges, and agreement on strategic direction for partnership work. This understanding forms the foundation for all subsequent work.

---

## **Stage 2: Strategic Definition and Priority-Setting**

### **Purpose and Activities**

The second stage moves from diagnosis to strategic planning. Based on the understanding developed in Stage 1, your leadership team defines strategic direction, identifies key priority areas, and begins planning for implementation.

This stage typically includes strategic planning sessions with your leadership team, development of diversity strategy and vision, identification of strategic pillars and objectives, definition of success metrics, and initial planning for how to organize and resource diversity work. We facilitate these strategic conversations, provide frameworks and best practices, help your team think through implications and trade-offs, and help build consensus around strategic direction.

This stage often includes board-level discussion and approval of diversity strategy, communication of strategy to organizational leadership and employees, and establishment of governance structures for overseeing diversity work.

### **Typical Deliverables**

This stage typically produces: a comprehensive diversity strategy document articulating vision, mission, and strategic pillars; a strategic roadmap for 2-3 years outlining key initiatives; governance structures and accountability frameworks; and a communication plan for rolling out diversity strategy.

### **Outcomes and Next Steps**

By the end of this stage, your organization has a clear, agreed-upon diversity strategy that has been communicated throughout the organization. There is organizational clarity about priorities and direction, and governance structures are in place to oversee implementation. Your organization is ready to move into active implementation.

---

## **Stage 3: Initiative Development and Implementation**

### **Purpose and Activities**

The third stage moves from planning to active implementation. Based on the strategy defined in Stage 2, your organization begins developing and implementing specific initiatives and programs aligned to strategic priorities.

This stage is typically the longest, often 6-12 months or longer depending on the scope of initiatives being implemented. During this stage, we work closely with your team on developing and implementing specific initiatives. This might include developing new recruitment strategies and training, launching leadership development programs, establishing employee resource groups, developing communication campaigns, implementing policy changes, or other initiatives aligned to your strategic priorities.

This stage typically involves intensive partnership with your HR team, regular strategic discussions about progress and challenges, facilitation of organizational conversations and decisions, direct participation in implementation activities, and regular monitoring and adjustment of initiatives.

### **Typical Activities During This Stage**

- Designing and launching 3-5 major initiatives aligned to strategic priorities
- Supporting recruitment specifically on diversity, or training internal teams on new recruitment approaches
- Designing and delivering leadership development programs
- Facilitating employee resource group development
- Developing communication and marketing approaches
- Implementing policy changes or process redesigns
- Building measurement and monitoring systems
- Providing ongoing coaching and support to organizational leaders and teams

### **Typical Challenges and How We Support**

Organizations typically encounter several types of challenges during this stage: resistance from some organizational stakeholders, difficulty maintaining momentum as work extends over time, resource constraints that limit implementation pace, complexity of managing multiple initiatives simultaneously, or difficulty seeing progress when results take time to materialize. We help your organization navigate these challenges through change management support, maintaining communication about progress, helping prioritize among initiatives, and helping build and sustain organizational momentum.

### **Outcomes and Next Steps**

By the end of this stage, your organization has implemented significant diversity and inclusion initiatives, established new processes and programs, begun to see results and impact, and built organizational capability and momentum around diversity and inclusion. The organization is positioned for continued progress and evolution of the agenda.

---

## **Stage 4: Consolidation, Monitoring, and Continuous Improvement**

### **Purpose and Activities**

The final stage focuses on consolidating progress, monitoring results, and building systems for continuous improvement. This stage reflects the evolution from implementing specific initiatives to embedding diversity into organizational culture, systems, and decision-making.

During this stage, we help your organization: evaluate effectiveness of initiatives implemented, gather feedback on progress and impact, refine approaches based on learning, plan for scaling or adjusting initiatives, establish ongoing measurement and monitoring systems, integrate diversity metrics into broader organizational performance measurement, build internal capability so that diversity work can be sustained with reduced external support.

This stage often involves a shift from external expert support to partnership where your internal teams take increasing responsibility, with external support available for specific challenges or new initiatives.

### **Typical Outcomes**

Organizations in this stage typically see: consolidated progress on diversity metrics, evidence of cultural shift toward greater inclusion, increased organizational capability to continue diversity work without external support, integration of diversity into organizational systems and decision-making, and establishment of diversity as an ongoing organizational priority rather than a time-limited initiative.

### **Evolution of Partnership**

The partnership often evolves during this stage. Rather than intensive monthly consulting support, your organization might shift to quarterly strategic discussions, occasional intensive support for new initiatives, or occasional coaching and advisory support. This shift reflects the successful building of organizational capability and the transition from external-led change to internally-led evolution.

---

## **A Continuous Journey**

It's important to recognize that this four-stage model represents a general progression, but that diversity and inclusion work is fundamentally a continuous journey rather than a destination. Organizations don't "complete" diversity work and then maintain status quo. Rather, as organizations evolve, as business conditions change, as social consciousness evolves, and as your organization learns more about what works, the diversity and inclusion agenda evolves.

Successful organizations maintain focus on diversity and inclusion as an ongoing organizational priority, continue to learn and evolve their approaches, remain open to feedback and new perspectives, and recognize that building inclusive organizations is a continuous journey rather than a project with an endpoint.
